Phone your rep is a tool designed to make it easy for constituents to contact their representatives. The target audience is broad, including anyone from teenagers to seniors citizens who wants to engage in politics.
Overview
Competitive Analysis
There are several tools out there to look up contact information for representatives. I investigated several and found that none of them satisfied all of our guiding design guidelines:
1. Accessibility: to ensure accessibility, we stayed compliant to the WCAG AAA contrast rules for text.
2. Mobile friendly: for a seamless flow from search to call, we wanted an optimized mobile experience. More specifically, this means making the phone number interactive so the user doesn’t need to copy and paste it.
3. Relevant media: none of the existing products linked to Instagram, a relevant link.
from ballotpedia.org (left) and commoncause.org (right)
Rapid Prototyping
Design kicked off with rapid low-fidelity wireframing to consider different ways we could present the information.

To align with the goal of being mobile friendly, all the designs include
- clickable phone number and call button
- clickable icon for getting directions

These designs, in comparision with existing sites, de-emphasize the photo and draw more attention to the name and contact information.
In addition, I separated search into 2 fields, zip code and representative name, due to a technical request.
Review and selection
The team felt that option 1 (all offices shown) was “overwhelming” when you see a list of several representatives. Though options 2 and 3 had less information, they still used a lot of space on the screen and would make some cells larger than other cells depeding on the number of offices the representative has (usually somewhere between 2 and 9 locations.)

We selected 4 to move forward because it is:
- easiest to scan the list of reps when they are all collapsed and equal height.
- less overwhelming amount of informaton up front
Next Iteration - High Fidelity
In high fidelity, I selected font colors and sizes that met accessibility standards for contrast.

To simplify the design, I maximized the data-ink ratio, by removing borders on the list items, increasing spacing, and swapping the call button out for an emphasized phone number link.

I also refined the purple to stand out more in comparison to the grey text.

Note- In the MVP, directions were not implemented.
Project Status
This project is currently in development by the Progressive Coders Network.
Further research
This version works successfully if you want to look up contact information. But it could use more educational context to help constituents evaluate their options for getting involved.

Is calling effective?
If someone has seven offices, which office should you call?
What issues is your representative voting on in the near future?

In a goal driven framing, you could make a query like “I care about ______. Who should I call?”

Or beyond “Who should I call?”, you could broaden the question to “What do I do?”

Certainly there are advocacy groups and in-person events in addition to the local officials you could call about this issue. How can we help people evaluate their options?
