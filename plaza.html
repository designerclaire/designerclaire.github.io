In the spring of 2017, Castle's local marketplace application, Plaza, was growing in popularity in Latin American developing markets. The Plaza team decided to target shop-owners in these markets as potential power users because of their large inventory. Before implementing this strategy, I travelled to the Dominican Republic to learn how shop-owners sell, advertise and organize their inventory in order to develop informed user personas.
Context
With my coworker Angel, I visited 16 local shops across the Dominican Republic to conduct a contextual inquiry (i.e. interviewing people about what they do while they are doing it). Angel, a DR native, assisted greatly in facilitating our store visits and communicating with interviewees.

During our shop visits, I recorded answers to a set of questions about sales, staffing and the use of technology. In addition, I asked participants to demonstrate common tasks and documented unexpected behaviors.
User research / contextual inquiry
During our visits, we saw a range of stores. Most relied on physical, hand written records while a few had simple desktop systems. Many used other channels to communicate with clientele, such as instant messaging and social media.

We observed that:

92% give discounts depending on the scenario

80% use pen and paper for receipts and sales tracking

46% do not accept credit cards, but several said they would like to

87% report communicating with clients via WhatsApp to send photos

53% post deals on a Facebook page

No stores use bar codes/scanners

Smaller stores allow familiar customers to pay in installments

Several stores use a code written on merchandise to indicate its sale price for negotiations

Most participants own modern smart phones for personal use but use outdated desktops, calculators and landlines for business

Implications
Our trip revealed several cultural blindspots to keep in mind while designing Plaza for users in the Dominican Republic, such as the expectation of flexible pricing and the ability to pay in parts. These findings also underscored the importance of enabling friendly digital payments, sales tracking and messaging between seller and buyer. Because I observed a ubiquity of smart phones and dusty, forgotten desktop computers, the study also suggests a mobile-first approach to innovation in this area.
